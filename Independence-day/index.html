1947

History of The Evolution of Pakistan’s Flag

Pakistan’s National Flag consists of a dark green field with a white crescent moon and a five-pointed star at its center, and a vertical white stripe at the hoist side. The flag was adopted on 11 August 1947, three days before Pakistan's independence.

The Journey of Pakistan's Independence

Pakistan’s journey to independence was marked by a long and arduous struggle for freedom, culminating in the creation of a new nation on August 14, 1947. The flag of Pakistan is not just a symbol of this new beginning, but also represents the aspirations and dreams of millions of Muslims who envisioned a homeland where they could live with dignity and freedom.

The Design of the Flag

The flag of Pakistan was designed by Amiruddin Kidwai, inspired by the flag of the All-India Muslim League. The dark green color symbolizes Islam and the Muslim majority of Pakistan, while the white stripe represents the religious minorities.

Symbolism of the Crescent and Star

The crescent on the flag symbolizes progress, while the five-pointed star represents light and knowledge. Together, these elements reflect Pakistan’s commitment to Islamic values and its aspirations for progress and enlightenment.

The Independence Movement

The demand for Pakistan grew under the leadership of Muhammad Ali Jinnah, who became the voice of Muslims in India. The movement for independence was fueled by the desire for a separate nation where Muslims could practice their religion freely and protect their cultural identity.

First Independence Day

On August 14, 1947, Pakistan emerged as an independent nation. The flag was hoisted for the first time, marking the birth of a new country. This day is celebrated every year with great enthusiasm and pride across Pakistan.

Founding Visionaries of Pakistan

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah and Allama Iqbal were instrumental in shaping the vision of Pakistan. Their leadership and ideas laid the foundation for a nation where Muslims could freely practice their faith and preserve their cultural identity.

Quaid-e-Azam’s Vision for Pakistan

Quaid-e-Azam Muhammad Ali Jinnah’s leadership was crucial in establishing Pakistan. He devoted his life to creating a homeland where Muslims could freely practice their religion and protect their cultural identity. His unwavering determination and vision led to the birth of an independent nation.

Allama Iqbal’s Vision for Pakistan

Allama Iqbal, the philosopher-poet, envisioned a separate homeland for Muslims in the subcontinent. He passionately advocated for a nation where Muslims could freely practice their faith and preserve their cultural heritage. His ideas and poetry inspired the movement that eventually led to Pakistan's creation.
